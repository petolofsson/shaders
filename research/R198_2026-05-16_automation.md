### CHROMA_STR from HWY_ACHROM_FRAC (slot 202)

**Verdict: FEASIBLE — but partial cancellation with R176 reduces effective range; subjective validation required before implementing.**

---

#### Signal characterization

`HWY_ACHROM_FRAC` (slot 202, `highway.fxh:35`) — fraction of pixels with Oklab C < 0.05, raw [0,1], no encoding needed (fits native UNORM).

EMA smoothing (`analysis_frame.fx:371-376`):
```hlsl
float alpha = lerp(saturate(frametime * 0.001), 1.0, scene_cut);
return float4(lerp(prev.r, out_C, alpha), ..., lerp(prev.a, achrom_frac, alpha));
```
At 60 fps (16.67 ms frametime): alpha ≈ 0.0167 → ~41-frame EMA half-life. Stable for gradual transitions. Scene cuts reset instantly (alpha = 1.0). Fluctuation risk from the job spec is adequately mitigated by the EMA.

Signal is **already read in the pipeline**: `inverse_grade.fx:40–41` uses it to lower the c_gate in near-all-neutral scenes:
```hlsl
float achrom_frac = ReadHWY(HWY_ACHROM_FRAC);
float c_gate      = lerp(0.10, 0.06, smoothstep(0.60, 0.85, achrom_frac));
```
ReadHWY infrastructure is proven for this slot.

#### Critical interaction: partial cancellation with R176

`HWY_ACHROM_FRAC` and `HWY_MEDIAN_C` are correlated by construction:

> When `achrom_frac > 0.5`, more than half of all pixels are below C=0.05, so the CDF 50th percentile (median_C) is necessarily below 0.05.

This means in the high-achrom regime — where the hypothesis most wants to reduce lift — R176's `chroma_adapt` is already at or near its maximum boost (1.25). The proposed `lerp(1.0, 0.70, achrom_frac)` factor partially cancels that boost rather than acting orthogonally.

Worked examples:

| achrom_frac | median_C (bound) | chroma_adapt (R176) | proposed factor | net multiplier |
|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|-----------------|----------------|
| 0.50        | ≤ 0.05          | ≈ 1.24              | 0.85            | **1.05×**      |
| 0.70        | ≤ 0.05          | ≈ 1.25              | 0.79            | **0.99×**      |
| 0.90        | ≤ 0.05          | ≈ 1.25              | 0.73            | **0.91×**      |
| 1.00        | ≤ 0.05          | ≈ 1.25              | 0.70            | **0.875×**     |

At achrom_frac = 0.70 the net is **essentially flat** — both effects cancel. Meaningful reduction (>8%) only appears above achrom_frac ≈ 0.87, which is an extreme scene (nearly monochrome).

In the low-achrom regime (achrom_frac < 0.5), where median_C can range above 0.05, the proposed factor provides genuine non-redundant "bimodal scene" information (many neutrals **plus** vivid colors), but the reduction is modest:

| achrom_frac | median_C (example) | chroma_adapt | proposed factor | net |
|-------------|-------------------|--------------|-----------------|-----|
| 0.40        | 0.10              | ≈ 1.09       | 0.88            | **0.96×** |
| 0.30        | 0.14              | ≈ 0.93       | 0.91            | **0.85×** |
| 0.10        | 0.18              | ≈ 0.85       | 0.97            | **0.82×** |

At low achrom_frac the scene is colorful throughout, and median_C is high — R176 is already pulling chroma_adapt down, so the proposed factor barely fires.

---

#### Conceptual contradiction with R176

R176 premise: *gray scenes need more chroma lift* (compensate for absent saturation).
Candidate premise: *gray scenes need less chroma lift* (avoid over-saturating the few colored elements).

These are opposite hypotheses about the same scene class. Both cannot be correct. R176 has been validated and committed; the candidate's premise requires subjective validation — specifically, testing whether high-achrom_frac frames exhibit over-saturation artifacts that R176's boost is causing.

Without that data, implementing the candidate risks embedding an assumption that directly opposes an already-committed design decision.

---

#### Implementation sketch (contingent on validation)

If subjective testing confirms over-saturation in high-achrom_frac frames, the cleanest insertion point is **after the R68A spatial attenuation** in `ApplyChroma`, keeping the two multipliers orthogonal:

`grade.fx:608–610` — current:
```hlsl
float chroma_str = ctx.chroma_str_base;
chroma_str *= lerp(1.0, 0.65, smoothstep(0.02, 0.08, local_var));  // R68A
```

Proposed addition (one new read, one multiply):
```hlsl
float chroma_str = ctx.chroma_str_base;
chroma_str *= lerp(1.0, 0.65, smoothstep(0.02, 0.08, local_var));  // R68A
chroma_str *= lerp(1.0, 0.70, ReadHWY(HWY_ACHROM_FRAC));           // achrom attenuation
```

Alternatively, if the decision is made to **replace** R176's boost with the achrom-based attenuation (resolving the conceptual contradiction by choosing one premise), both would be consolidated in `BuildSceneCtx`. That is a larger change requiring a separate research pass.

Note: `HWY_ACHROM_FRAC` is [0,1] and requires no decode scaling. One additional `ReadHWY` call per pixel (tex2Dlod on a 1-texel texture — negligible cost).

---

#### Recommendation

**Do not implement yet.** Two blockers:

1. **Partial cancellation**: effective reduction is 0–12.5% in the achrom_frac > 0.5 regime (where it most matters), not the 30% suggested by the formula. The formula as written needs to be stronger — e.g., `lerp(1.0, 0.40, achrom_frac)` — or the R176 boost range needs to be narrowed to make room.

2. **Premise conflict with R176**: subjective A/B test needed. Record a high-achrom_frac scene (overcast interior, fog, snow), compare R176-boosted vs. achrom-attenuated output. If the boost is beneficial (makes sparse colors pop), R176 is correct and this candidate should be closed. If the boost causes over-saturation artifacts, R176's upper range should be trimmed and this candidate implemented.
